## Q1

### Define Production Function and explain.

A **production function** shows the relationship between inputs and output. It explains the maximum output that a firm can produce by using different quantities of factors like labour and capital during a given period.

The general form is:

**Q = f(L, K)**

Where:
- **Q** = output
- **L** = labour
- **K** = capital

It helps a firm understand how output changes when input changes. For example, if the number of workers or machines increases, production may increase. The production function is useful in production planning, cost control, and efficient use of resources. It is an important concept because it shows the technical relationship between input and output in a simple way.

## Q2

### Explain Law of Variable Proportions.

The **Law of Variable Proportions** explains what happens to output when one input is increased while other inputs remain fixed.

It has **three stages**:
- **Stage 1: Increasing returns**. Output increases at an increasing rate.
- **Stage 2: Diminishing returns**. Output increases, but at a decreasing rate.
- **Stage 3: Negative returns**. Output starts falling.

For example, if more labour is added to fixed land, production first rises fast, then slowly, and finally falls. This happens because the fixed factor becomes overused.

This law is important because it helps firms decide the most efficient level of input use. The second stage is considered the rational stage of production.

## Q3

### Discuss Isoquants.

An **isoquant** is a curve that shows different combinations of labour and capital that produce the same level of output.

**Features of isoquants:**
- they slope **downward** from left to right
- they are **convex to the origin**
- two isoquants **never intersect**
- a higher isoquant shows a **higher level of output**
- they usually do not touch the axes

Isoquants help a producer choose different input combinations for the same production level. For example, a firm may use more labour and less capital, or more capital and less labour, but still produce the same output.

Thus, isoquants are useful in understanding substitution between factors of production and efficient resource use.

## Q4

### Discuss Isocost curves.

An **isocost curve** shows different combinations of labour and capital that a firm can buy with a fixed amount of money.

Its equation is:

**C = wL + rK**

Where:
- **C** = total cost
- **w** = wage rate
- **L** = labour
- **r** = rent of capital
- **K** = capital

An isocost line is a **straight line** because the trade-off between labour and capital is constant at given prices. Points on the line show combinations the firm can afford. Points inside the line are affordable, and points outside are not possible with the same budget.

Isocost curves are important because they help the producer find the least-cost combination of inputs.

## Q5

### Explain Producer’s Equilibrium.

**Producer’s equilibrium** is the point where a firm gets maximum output at minimum cost, or produces a given output at the lowest possible cost.

It occurs when an **isoquant is tangent to an isocost line**. At this point:

**MRTS = w/r**

This means the marginal rate of technical substitution is equal to the ratio of factor prices.

At equilibrium, the producer has no reason to change the combination of labour and capital. Any other combination would either increase cost or reduce output efficiency. So this point is considered the best input combination for the firm.

Producer’s equilibrium is useful in production planning and cost minimization.

**Diagram to draw:** Draw an **isoquant touching an isocost line** at one point.

## Q6

### Explain MRTS.

**MRTS** means **Marginal Rate of Technical Substitution**. It shows the rate at which one input can be substituted for another without changing the level of output.

In simple words, it tells how much capital can be reduced when one more unit of labour is used, while keeping output the same.

It is studied with the help of an isoquant curve. MRTS usually **diminishes** as more and more units of one factor are used. That is why isoquants are convex to the origin.

MRTS is important because it helps producers choose the best combination of labour and capital. It also helps in finding producer’s equilibrium when MRTS becomes equal to the price ratio of inputs.

## Q7

### Discuss Fixed Cost and Variable Cost.

**Fixed cost** is the cost that remains the same even when output changes. It does not depend on the level of production.

Examples:
- rent
- insurance
- salary of permanent staff

**Variable cost** changes according to the level of output. When production increases, variable cost also increases.

Examples:
- raw materials
- electricity
- packing cost

Both are important in business decisions. Fixed cost must be paid even if production is low. Variable cost changes directly with production. A firm must understand both costs to fix price, calculate profit, and control expenses properly.

## Q8

### Discuss Explicit Cost and Implicit Cost.

**Explicit cost** means actual money payments made by the firm to outsiders for production.

Examples:
- wages
- rent
- raw materials
- electricity bill

**Implicit cost** means the value of self-owned resources used in business without direct cash payment.

Examples:
- owner’s own building used for business
- owner’s own labour or managerial service

Explicit cost is recorded in accounts, but implicit cost is not directly paid. Both are important in real business analysis because they help in finding true profit. If only explicit cost is considered, the actual cost of business may appear lower than it really is.

## Q9

### Discuss Cost and Opportunity Cost.

**Cost** means the expenditure incurred for producing goods or services. It includes spending on labour, materials, rent, power, and other resources.

**Opportunity cost** is the value of the next best alternative that is sacrificed when one choice is made.

For example, if a building owned by a businessman is used for his own shop instead of giving it on rent, the rent he could have earned is the opportunity cost.

Opportunity cost is important because every business decision involves choosing one option and giving up another. So, managers should consider not only actual money cost but also the benefit lost from the alternative use of resources.

This concept helps in better planning and smarter decision-making.

## Q10

### Explain Break-Even Point (BEP) with graph.

**Break-Even Point (BEP)** is the level of sales where **total revenue equals total cost**. At this point, there is **no profit and no loss**.

The formula is:

**BEP = Fixed Cost / (Selling Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit)**

Example:
- Fixed cost = 20,000
- Selling price = 100
- Variable cost = 60

So,
**BEP = 20,000 / (100 - 60) = 500 units**

This means the firm must sell 500 units to cover all costs. Sales below this point give loss, and sales above this point give profit. BEP is useful for planning sales targets and cost control.

## Q11

### Explain importance of BEP.

The **Break-Even Point** is important because it tells the firm the minimum sales needed to avoid loss.

**Importance of BEP:**
- helps in fixing **sales targets**
- helps in **profit planning**
- helps in **cost control**
- helps in comparing **different production plans**
- supports **pricing decisions**
- shows the level of **risk** in business

When managers know the break-even point, they can decide how much to produce and sell. It also helps them understand how changes in cost or price affect profit. So, BEP is a simple but very useful tool in business planning and financial decision-making.

## Q12

### Discuss BEP with formulas and graph.

**Break-Even Point (BEP)** is the point where total cost and total revenue are equal. At this point, profit is zero.

**Formulas:**
- **BEP in units = Fixed Cost / Contribution per unit**
- **Contribution per unit = Selling Price per Unit - Variable Cost per Unit**

So,
**BEP = Fixed Cost / (SP - VC)**

This formula helps the firm find the number of units it must sell to avoid loss. BEP is useful in cost-volume-profit analysis, profit planning, and pricing decisions.

In the graph, the **Total Revenue line** and **Total Cost line** intersect at one point. That point is the break-even point. Left side of the point shows loss, and right side shows profit.
