# Concept
*The continuum concept in thermodynamics assumes that matter is continuously*
*distributed in space*, ignoring its molecular nature. Under this assumption, properties Such as pressure, temperature, density, and velocity are considered to be well-defined at every point in the system.

---
### Why continuum concept is used?

- Simplifies thermodynamic and fluid-flow analysis
- avoids complex molecular-level calculations
- Makes the macroscopic approach possible.
---
### Mean free path ($\lambda$)  and characteristic length(L) 
**Characteristic length (L)** is *the typical length scale of a system*.
**Mean free path** is *the average distance travelled by the particles before collision.*

_A system can be treated as a continuum when:_
**Characteristic length L $≫$ Mean free path λ**
• This is often expressed using the Knudsen number: $kn=\frac{\lambda}{L}$

---
#### Knudsen values:
• **Kn < 0.01** → _Continuum assumption valid_
• **0.01 < Kn < 0.1** → _Slip flow_
• **0.1 < Kn < 10** → _Transition flow_
• **Kn > 10** → _Free molecular flow_
### $\lambda$ values: 
Mean Free Path (λ) – Typical Values
• **Solids**: _λ ≈ 10$^{−10}$ m_ (very small)
• **Liquids**: _λ ≈ 10$^{−9}$m_
• **Gases** (air at STP): _λ ≈ 6.5 × 10$^{−8}$ m_(≈ 0.065μm)

---
### The validity Check
The continuum **assumption is valid** *when The mean free path (λ) of molecules is very small compared to the characteristic length(L)* of the system.
- This condition is usually satisfied in Liquids, Solids and Gases at normal pressure and temperature.
#### But it fails in:
- High vacuum conditions
- Rarefied gases
- Micro- and nanoscale flows
